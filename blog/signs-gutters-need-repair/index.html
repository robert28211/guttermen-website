7 Signs Your Gutters Need Repair

Some problems a cleanout won't fix. Here's how to tell what's actually going on with your gutters.

A clogged gutter overflows during rain. A damaged gutter overflows, leaks, sags, or fails even when it's clear. The two problems look similar from the ground but have different fixes. Here are seven signs your gutters have crossed from "needs cleaning" into "needs repair."

1. Water Overflowing Between Downspouts During Rain

If your gutters are clean but still overflow mid-section during heavy rain, the gutter is either undersized for your roof area or pitched incorrectly. Water should flow toward downspouts, not pool and spill over the front edge. The fix is re-pitching the gutter or adding a downspout — not cleaning it again.

2. Visible Sagging or Pulling Away From the Fascia

Gutters sag when hangers fail, when the fascia board behind them rots and can no longer hold fasteners, or when debris weight has bent the gutter over time. A sagging gutter doesn't drain correctly even when it's clear. This is a repair job — new hangers, possibly new fascia, sometimes a section replacement.

3. Leaking at Seams or Corners

Sectional gutters develop leaks at the joints as the sealant degrades, especially in SC's heat and humidity. Seamless gutters can develop leaks at corners and downspout connections. A dripping seam during rain is a repair — resealing or section replacement — not a cleanout issue.

4. Rust Stains or Orange Streaking on the Gutter Exterior

Orange or rust-colored streaks on the outside of the gutter indicate that water is sitting inside rather than draining. Standing water corrodes aluminum gutters from the inside and signals a drainage problem — wrong pitch, blocked downspout connection, or a low spot caused by a bent section.

5. Peeling Paint or Water Staining on Fascia and Siding

Paint peeling off the fascia directly below the gutter line, or staining streaks running down the siding, means water is getting behind or around the gutter instead of through it. This can be a leak at the back edge, failed caulking, or a gutter that's pulling away slightly and creating a gap.

6. Mold or Mildew Growth on the Fascia Board

Mold on the fascia behind the gutter is a sign of chronic moisture — water is consistently getting into a place it shouldn't. Left alone, this progresses to rot, which means replacing not just the gutter but the structural wood behind it. Catching it early is significantly cheaper.

7. Cracks, Holes, or Visible Damage to the Gutter Body

Cracks and holes from impact (falling branches, ladders), frost heave, or just age can't be cleaned away. Small holes can sometimes be patched; larger damage usually means section replacement. If you're seeing multiple cracks across several sections, it's worth a conversation about whether full replacement is the better value.

Repair or Replace?

Repair makes sense when damage is isolated — one bad section, failed hangers, a leaking seam. Replacement makes more sense when damage is widespread across multiple sections, when the gutters are very old, or when the existing system is fundamentally undersized for the roof.
