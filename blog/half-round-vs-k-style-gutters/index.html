Half-Round vs. K-Style Gutters: Which Is Right for Your Home?

Two proven gutter profiles — different shapes, different strengths. Here's how to choose.

What Each Profile Looks Like

K-style gutters have a flat back, flat bottom, and a decorative ogee (S-curve) profile on the front face that resembles crown molding. They're the dominant profile on American homes built since the 1960s — you'll recognize them on almost every house in a standard subdivision.

Half-round gutters are exactly what they sound like: a smooth semicircular trough. No flat bottom, no decorative front. They were the standard before K-style displaced them, and they're still the correct choice for historic homes, high-end architectural work, and applications where maximum flow capacity matters.

Capacity and Clog Resistance

K-style gutters hold more water per inch of width than half-round — their flat bottom and angular sides give them greater volume for a given face width. A 5-inch K-style gutter outperforms a 5-inch half-round on raw volume.

But half-round gutters have a significant advantage on debris: the smooth curved interior has no corners or flat surfaces where debris settles and packs. Water and debris flush through more naturally. For homes with heavy pine needle or organic debris load, that smooth interior means fewer clogs between cleanouts.

Which One Fits Your Home

Choose K-Style if…Choose Half-Round if…
Your home is standard modern constructionYour home is historic, craftsman, or high-end architectural
Budget is a primary concernYou want maximum flow capacity and clog resistance
You want to match existing gutters on the homeYou're replacing everything and want the best long-term solution
Your roofline is simple and relatively low-pitchYour roof is steep, large, or sheds water fast in heavy rain

Cost Difference

Half-round gutters cost more than K-style — the profile requires different fabrication and the hangers are different. In aluminum, expect half-round to run roughly 15–25% higher per linear foot than equivalent K-style. For most homes, that difference is a few hundred dollars total — worth it for the right application, not necessary if K-style suits the home.
