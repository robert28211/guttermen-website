Frequently Asked Questions

Everything South Carolina homeowners ask us before, during, and after their gutter project.

Repair makes sense when the problem is isolated — a single leaking seam, a few loose hangers, or one sagging section. We can reseal a joint, replace a hanger, or re-pitch a section in a single visit. Replacement becomes the better call when damage spans multiple sections, the gutter material is compromised (rust, severe denting, corrosion), the fascia behind the gutter has rotted and needs to come down anyway, or the existing gutters are undersized for your roofline. We assess on-site and give you a straight answer — we won't recommend replacement when repair is the right call, and we won't keep patching a system that's past saving.

Three common causes: undersized gutters, improper pitch, or too few downspouts. Standard 4-inch gutters are often too small for South Carolina's rainfall intensity — SC summer storms routinely drop 2–4 inches per hour, which overwhelms 4-inch gutters on larger roof sections. Gutters also need to be pitched at least 1/16 inch per foot toward the downspout; if they're level or pitched the wrong direction, water pools instead of flowing. Finally, if your home has a large roof area draining into too few downspouts, the system simply can't move the volume fast enough. An on-site assessment can identify which issue is causing the problem.

Most SC homes need at least two cleanouts per year — once in spring after winter storms, and once in late fall after leaves have fully dropped. However, homes with significant pine tree coverage often need 3–4 cleanouts annually because pine needles shed year-round. Pine needles are particularly problematic because they're narrow enough to slip past many gutter guards and pack into a dense mat that blocks water flow entirely. If your gutters are overflowing during summer thunderstorms, they likely need a mid-season cleanout as well.

Watch for: pooling water against the foundation after rain, cracks in foundation or basement walls, doors or windows that stick or won't close properly, white mineral deposits (efflorescence) on basement walls, and soil erosion along the foundation line. South Carolina's red clay soil makes this especially damaging — clay holds water against the foundation and expands when wet, creating pressure that accelerates cracking. If you notice any of these signs, address the gutter system first before investing in foundation repair, since the repair won't hold if the water source isn't corrected.

Yes — indirectly but reliably. When gutters overflow or leak at seams, water runs behind the fascia boards and into the soffit, where it saturates insulation and wood framing. In South Carolina's humidity, that moisture rarely dries out — mold establishes itself quickly in warm, enclosed, wet spaces. You may notice dark streaks on exterior walls, peeling paint near the roofline, or musty odors in attic spaces. By the time you see visible mold or smell it inside, the damage behind the wall has usually been developing for months. Keeping gutters clear and properly sealed is the most cost-effective way to prevent moisture intrusion at the roofline.

The most common cause is fastener failure. Older gutter systems used spike-and-ferrule fasteners driven into the fascia board — over years of thermal expansion and contraction, those spikes work loose. Additionally, if the fascia board behind the gutter has begun to rot (often from water caused by the leaking gutter itself), there's no longer solid wood for the fasteners to grip. Gutter weight from packed debris also accelerates the problem. The fix is to remove the failed fasteners, assess the fascia condition, replace any rotted wood, and reinstall with modern hidden hanger brackets screwed directly into the rafter tails — a much more secure attachment that won't loosen over time.

5-inch K-style is the standard for most SC homes. 4-inch is an older, narrower profile that often can't handle SC's rainfall intensity. 6-inch gutters are a commercial-grade option that handles dramatically more volume and clogs less frequently — worth it for homes with steep roof pitches, large roof sections, or heavy tree coverage. The deciding factor is the drainage area each gutter run serves: a long run over a large square footage benefits from 6-inch. We assess your specific roof geometry during the quote visit and recommend the right size. Upsizing where it matters costs relatively little upfront and can eliminate years of overflow problems.

It depends entirely on the type of guard and your debris situation. Cheap plastic screen guards are largely ineffective — they clog with small debris and create more problems than they solve. Quality micro-mesh guards are genuinely effective at keeping most debris out while allowing water through. However, no gutter guard is 100% maintenance-free in SC, where pine needles are prevalent. Pine needles are narrow enough to work through or around many guards over time. The honest answer: a quality guard system significantly reduces cleanout frequency (often from 2–3 times per year to once every 2–4 years), but doesn't eliminate maintenance entirely. It's worth the investment for homes with heavy tree coverage where annual cleanouts are a recurring expense.

Corner joints are the most stress-prone point in any sectional gutter system — two pieces of metal meet where water volume and velocity are highest. The sealant at these joints breaks down over time, and in South Carolina, UV exposure and heat accelerate this significantly. Corner sealant that might last 7–10 years in a cooler climate often fails in 3–5 years here. Signs of a leaking corner: staining or peeling paint directly below a corner, water damage to the fascia at that point, or visible water running down the corner during rain. Corner resealing is typically a fast, affordable repair — but ignored, the concentrated water flow can cause significant fascia and soffit damage quickly.

Quality seamless aluminum gutters typically last 15–25 years in South Carolina — somewhat shorter than the 20–30 years you'd get in a cooler, drier climate. The factors that shorten gutter life here: UV and heat degrading sealant at joints, pine needle and debris load adding weight stress, hurricane-force winds, and high humidity keeping metal wet more of the year. Cheap sectional gutters with lots of joints will fail significantly sooner. The best ways to extend gutter life in SC: seamless construction (fewer failure points), regular cleanouts (removes weight and moisture), prompt repair of any developing issues, and gutter guards if you have heavy tree coverage.

Yes — almost always. Best practice calls for downspout extensions that carry water at least 4–6 feet away from the foundation, directed toward a slope or drain. Downspouts that terminate at the foundation are one of the most common causes of foundation water intrusion we see. The fix is straightforward: surface extensions can be added, and underground discharge pipes that have silted up can be inspected and cleared or bypassed. We assess downspout termination during every gutter visit because solving the gutter problem without addressing where the water goes doesn't actually protect your foundation.
